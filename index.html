40 sky cultures · 1,068 stars · one shared sky

Research Questions

  1. Do constellations across different cultures share stars, and how?
  2. Which star patterns appear most often across cultures?
  3. How do shapes, star usage, and naming conventions differ and overlap?
  4. Which sky regions are represented more or less densely across cultures?
  5. Can constellations be meaningfully clustered by semantic theme?
  6. How does constellation complexity vary across cultures?

The same stars,
different stories

People across the world, and throughout different time periods, have looked at the same night sky and told completely different stories about it. The same stars that one culture connected into a hunter, another saw as a fishing net, and another as a cardinal point or a spirit.

These constellation systems are not just astronomical records — they are windows into how different peoples across time and geography understood the world around them. Our dataset, compiled by Dr. Doina Bucur, covers 40 sky cultures spanning from roughly 1700 to the present, with ~500 constellation entries across every inhabited continent.

By combining geospatial, network, and interactive star-coordinate visualizations, we reveal both the universal patterns and the deep cultural differences encoded in the night sky.

Finding 01

Animals dominate globally

Across nearly every region and era, animal-type constellations make up the largest semantic category — but the specific animals vary dramatically by geography and biome.

Finding 02

Certain stars are universal anchors

A small set of bright stars appear in the constellations of many unrelated cultures — Orion's belt being the most prominent cross-cultural reference point across the dataset.

Finding 03

Europe skews toward large constellations

European sky cultures (Ruelle, Dien, Romania) show disproportionately high animal counts and more complex multi-star figures, while many indigenous cultures favor abstract groupings.

Finding 04

Abstract themes rise in the Americas

Americas cultures show a notably higher share of abstract and group-type constellations compared to Asian or European counterparts — a pattern visible across multiple cultures and eras.

Current Visualizations

The Full Picture

Three interactive views that together answer our six research questions — each designed for a different analytical lens.

Interactive · HTML

Figure 1

Geographic Origins & Semantic Distributions

Maps 40 cultures to their geographic origins with bubble size encoding constellation count. Select a region or click a culture to reveal its semantic breakdown — proportions of animal, human, object, nature, and abstract constellation types. A time-period slider filters cultures by documentation era, exposing how themes shift from pre-1900 to contemporary fieldwork.

Interactive · Kumu

Figure 2

Constellation Relationship Network

A force-directed network mapping the relationships between cultures and their shared semantic categories. Clusters reveal which cultures group together by constellation theme, and which stand alone as outliers. Dense nodes represent semantic categories claimed by many cultures; sparse edges point to culturally unique naming traditions.

Interactive · HTML

Figure 3

The Night Sky as Seen by 40 Cultures

Plots 1,068 stars by right ascension and declination — actual sky coordinates. Select any culture to see its constellation lines rendered in gold. In Show All mode, click any star to reveal every culture that incorporated it, with each constellation name clickable to highlight that specific figure in orange. Reveals which stars serve as cross-cultural anchors.

Design Evolution

Previous Iterations

Earlier versions of the visualizations that shaped our analytical approach and informed the final designs above.

Figure 1 · v1 · Power BI

Geospatial — Initial Prototype

The first geospatial prototype built in Power BI. Established the core idea of mapping cultures to geographic origins with semantic breakdowns — but lacked the interactivity and custom filtering of the final HTML version.

Figure 2 · v1 · Gephi

Star Network — Initial Prototype

The original network visualization built in Gephi, mapping stars as nodes and constellation co-membership as edges. Revealed density patterns and hub stars but was static and hard to explore. Evolved into the interactive Kumu and star-map visualizations.

On iteration: Both initial prototypes confirmed our core hypotheses but exposed limitations — Power BI's geospatial capabilities couldn't support the semantic filtering we needed, and Gephi's static export made star-level exploration impossible. The pivot to HTML and Kumu allowed us to build the interactivity that the data demands.
